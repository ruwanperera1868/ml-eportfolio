Final Reflection

This module gave me a structured but progressively more demanding journey through machine learning. At the beginning, the emphasis was on understanding what machine learning is, how it fits into Industry 4.0, and why data quality, algorithm selection and ethical awareness matter. As the module progressed, the work became increasingly practical through exploratory data analysis, regression, clustering, neural networks and finally broader questions of deployment, evaluation and future impact.

The most important shift in my learning was moving from seeing machine learning as a set of techniques to understanding it as a full decision-making process. In the early units, I focused on methods in a procedural way: run an analysis, inspect a result, and report the output. Over time I became more critical. I began to ask whether the data was suitable, whether the model was interpretable, whether the result was reliable, and whether it could be justified in a real organisational context. This was especially clear in the Airbnb project where regression and clustering were both useful, but in different ways. Regression helped explain price behaviour, while clustering supported segmentation and practical decision-making. That made me realise that model usefulness is not identical to model accuracy.

The technical activities helped me build confidence step by step. Exploratory data analysis reinforced the importance of understanding variables before modelling. Correlation and regression clarified the difference between association and prediction. Clustering demonstrated that useful insights can emerge even when labels do not exist. The perceptron and gradient descent tasks made neural networks feel less abstract because I could see how learning rates and iterations affected behaviour. The CNN work extended that understanding into deep learning and showed both the strengths and limitations of image classification. Seeing confusion across visually similar classes was valuable because it highlighted that strong performance metrics do not remove uncertainty.

The collaborative and ethical elements were equally important. The discussions on Industry 4.0 and AI writing tools pushed me beyond code and outputs. They forced me to think about fairness, misuse, accountability and the professional responsibility of machine learning practitioners. I found that my own perspective became more balanced during the module. Initially, I was strongly focused on capability and outcomes. By the end, I was far more alert to questions of transparency, bias and governance. In practical terms, that means I now see model evaluation as including both technical and ethical judgement.

Working as part of a team also developed my professional awareness. It required coordination, contribution tracking, negotiation over analytical choices and the ability to align technical work with a shared business question. Comparing the Unit 6 proposal with the final Unit 11 project made it clear that project work evolves significantly once a team begins interacting with real data. Some early assumptions proved too simplistic, and the final output became stronger because the analysis matured. That experience was useful because it resembled real development work where requirements change as understanding improves.

Overall, the module has strengthened my understanding of machine learning algorithms, but more importantly it has improved how I think about applying them. I now approach problems with greater structure: understand the data, question the assumptions, choose methods for a reason, evaluate critically, and consider wider impact. That mindset is the most valuable outcome of the module and it is what I would carry forward into professional practice.
